# THE LONG HELLO — a playthrough, written as a story

> **Handoff note.** This is one complete, typical playthrough of THE LONG HELLO,
> transcribed as continuous prose rather than played. All lines of text are the
> game's actual prose; the connective tissue ("you sit," "you stop at the park")
> narrates the player's inputs. Choices assumed here are the *attentive* path the
> game was tuned around: the player sits with every patient, stops asking the
> object questions early, discovers the hum on night 21, earns the second message
> on night 43, and leaves joan's hat. Where the game randomizes or gates content,
> this transcript takes the intended/typical branch. Player-variable things:
> the dialogue choices, whether/when they sit, what they leave at the base
> (coffee / a button / the hat / a written line), whether the second message is
> ever earned at all (the ending changes one line on this), and whether they idled
> on the title screen (which earns the final callback line). The game's register
> is all lowercase except the inscription, the news ticker, and scene headers.

---

The title screen says only: **T H E  L O N G  H E L L O**. The player does
nothing for a minute, and the game notices: *there is no hurry.* Then they begin.

---

## NIGHT 1

the ward is quiet.

the strip light in the corridor is failing again. it beats like a slow pulse.

mr. okafor is awake.

You sit with him. The game takes the controls away for this — there is nothing
to do but be there.

you sit.
the machines tick.
he doesn't need you to say anything.
minutes pass.

he sleeps. you stay a while anyway.

the shift is over.

**MORNING.** the canal path. the water holds the streetlights flat.

the flat: the kettle. the window. the bed. You look out: the city from four
floors up. you can't see the park from here. you know exactly where it is anyway.

sleep comes like a tide: eventually, then all at once.

## NIGHT 2

mr. okafor is awake. he has his glasses on. there is nothing to read.

"thirty-one years i taught geography," he says. "capitals. rivers. i can still
do every capital."

"do you think we're alone? i always hoped."

You say nothing. he looks at you for a long time. "yes," he says. "me too."

You pull the chair closer. he doesn't open his eyes. he knows the sound of that
chair. You sit until a bell down the hall calls the ward back. his breathing
settles into something like rest.

the shift is over. The walk home. The kettle. Sleep.

## NIGHT 3

mr. okafor is asleep. his breathing has changed.

you know this music.

you sit. his lips are moving. you don't lean in. some things aren't yours.

at six, his daughter arrives with coffee she won't drink. you give her the chair.

the shift is over.

**MORNING.** the canal path. the water holds the streetlights flat.

there is something in the park.

        .------.
      /          \
     |            |
     |            |
     |            |

it stands in the grass like it has stood there always. taller than a door.

the color of wet pewter.

a crowd, at dawn, in coats over pyjamas. nobody talks above a murmur.

police tape, already sagging.

a helicopter circles. even it sounds unsure.

A news ticker starts at the bottom of the screen and never quite stops:
**OBJECTS REPORTED IN — PARIS. LAGOS. CHENNAI. EVERYWHERE.**

you stand with the crowd until the cold finds you.

home. bed. the ceiling, for a long time.

## NIGHT 4

mr. okafor's bed is empty. the window is open. someone has opened it on purpose.

the bed is made by nine. by ten there will be a new name on the door. that is
the job.

the ward manager wants everyone to watch a briefing about "the objects." nobody
watches it.

mrs. b in room four needs turning. you turn her.

You check the charts — the charts say what the charts said an hour ago. you
initial them — and finish the rounds. the shift is over.

*(the ticker, running underneath the days now:)*
**OBJECTS NOW CONFIRMED ON SIX CONTINENTS. COUNT UNKNOWN.**
**NO TWO ACCOUNTS OF THE ARRIVAL AGREE. MOST WITNESSES REPORT: "IT WAS JUST THERE."**

**MORNING.** the walk home is longer when someone has died. or you walk it
slower. one of those.

You stop at the park — the first morning it's possible to.

the tape is down already. nobody voted on this; it happened. the crowd has made
itself a ring, at a distance nobody agreed out loud. a man is filming it,
narrating quietly, as if it might overhear.

you come to it the way you come off a bad shift: emptied.

you put your hand on it without deciding to.

warm. of course. it is always warm.

you stand there a while. nobody asks you anything.

neither does it.

your phone is in your hand before you notice deciding. everyone else's is up
already, a little congregation of screens. You put the phone away. the egg
looks better at first hand. most things do.

You walk around it. you step over the flattened grass and walk. the words wrap
the whole waist of it. you have to walk to read them.

**WE WISH YOU TO KNOW**
**THAT YOU ARE NOT ALONE IN THE UNIVERSE**
**AND WE MEAN NO HARM.**

then again, in other letters. all the way around. the same. you come back to
where you started.

You walk home. you don't remember the stairs. the kettle boils. you forget the
tea. you boil it again. sleep.

## NIGHT 5

the new name on mr. okafor's door is edith.

edith is ninety-one. she looks at you the way a hawk looks at weather.

"you're the night one," she says. "good. the day ones hover."

you take the chair. she pretends not to notice. she notices everything. You sit
a while. "you'll do," she says, to the ceiling.

**ESTIMATE REVISED: 400,000. NOT ONE IN A ROADWAY.**
**EVERY OBJECT CARRIES THE SAME INSCRIPTION, IN THE LANGUAGE OF WHERE IT STANDS.**

**MORNING.** At the park, a man at the ring of grass turns to you. "is it warm?"
he asks. he hasn't touched it. lots of them haven't. as if it needs an
appointment.

"yes. like a hand," you tell him.

he looks at it a long moment. then he goes and touches it. so does the woman
behind him. and her daughter.

You touch it yourself before you go. you touch it. it is warm. Home. Sleep.

## NIGHT 6

edith, on the news: "in my day the sky minded its own business."

she says it like a joke. she watches you to see if it lands like one. You laugh.
"good," she says. "someone here has sense."

you sit. she deals imaginary cards on the blanket, an old habit with nowhere to
go. "same time tomorrow," she says, like she's doing you the favor.

**"IMPOSSIBLE TO FAKE" — MATERIALS PANEL. HOAX THEORIES COLLAPSE.**
**SEISMOGRAPHS RECORDED NOTHING. THEY WERE NOT DROPPED. THEY WERE SET DOWN.**

**MORNING.** At the park: someone has dragged a bench nearer to it. municipal.
possibly stolen. nobody minds.

you sit on the bench. For the first time, the player just — stops. The game
counts stillness; every keypress resets it, deflected with small lines: *a
jogger passes. a dog noses the bench leg, moves on. wind moves the canal trees.*
The player holds still instead, until: the cold gets into your coat. time to go.

## NIGHT 7

edith's hands work at the hem of the blanket, pleating it, letting it go.

"i had four sisters," she says. "i'm the last one holding the thread."

you sit where she can see you without turning her head. her hands go quiet on
the blanket, one on top of the other.

**UN EMERGENCY SESSION ENTERS THIRD DAY. STATEMENT PROMISED. STATEMENT DELAYED.**

**MORNING.** rain, thin as breath. the canal wears rings. You stop at the park
anyway.

you touch it. it is warm. the rain does not bead on it the way it should.

You sit in the rain until the cold wins. Home. Sleep.

## NIGHT 8

edith is tired tonight. the hawk is folded down to something smaller.

"sit where i can see you," she says. "don't fuss."

you sit where she can see you. you don't fuss. "there," she says, as if
something has been settled.

**CROWDS DEMAND RESPONSE. OBJECTS SILENT.**
**CONTACT PROTOCOLS ATTEMPTED IN 21 COUNTRIES: MATHEMATICS. MUSIC. LIGHTS. NOTHING.**

**MORNING.** At the park, a new verb has appeared, and the player tries it.

you ask it what it wants.

the metal says nothing.

it is not a refusal. it is patience.

You sit a while instead, then walk home.

## NIGHT 9

edith's bed has been stripped. the sheets are very white.

the thread, let go.

the corridor light has been fixed. it is worse, somehow, steady.

**LEAKED FOOTAGE: SOLDIER FIRES ON OBJECT. ROUND VANISHES. NO RESPONSE.**

**MORNING.** the walk home is longer when someone has died. or you walk it
slower. one of those.

At the park: you come to it the way you come off a bad shift: emptied. you put
your hand on it without deciding to. warm. of course. it is always warm. you
stand there a while. nobody asks you anything. neither does it.

you don't remember the stairs. the kettle boils. you forget the tea. you boil
it again.

## NIGHT 10

the new name is marisol. she is fifty-four. the room smells of the lilies her
sister brings.

she shows you a photograph of a boy on a bicycle.

"my son. he's camping out at one of them, him and half the city."

"he was going to be an engineer," she says, as if the two facts argue.

you sit. she holds the photograph face-down on the blanket, keeping it warm.
she is asleep before the hour turns.

**NO RETALIATION REPORTED ANYWHERE. ANALYSTS: "THERE IS NO FLOOR."**

**MORNING.** At the park the crowd is thinner. a man sells coffee from a cart
now. the cart has an egg painted on it already.

you ask it why here. the warm says nothing back.

That's the last question the player ever asks it. From here on, they only sit.

## NIGHT 11

marisol can't sleep. she talks about her garden. the slugs. the war on the slugs.

"you can't win," she says, happily.

you sit and hear about the year the foxes got in. a good year, by the sound of
it. "anyway," she says. "anyway." and sleeps.

**DESERT FOOTAGE AUTHENTICATED: ORDNANCE TEST. OBJECT UNMOVED. SAND FUSED TO GLASS.**

**MORNING.** The bench. Stillness. the moment thins. a bicycle bell, far off.
the cold gets into your coat. time to go.

## NIGHT 12

marisol's son came at last. you see the bicycle boy in the face of a grown man
asleep in the chair.

she watches him sleep. you check her drip quietly, so as not to wake either of
them.

the chair is taken tonight. good.

**MARKETS STEADY. "AN EVENT WITH NO DEADLINE IS HARD TO PRICE."**
**PLACES OF WORSHIP REPORT RECORD ATTENDANCE. NO CONSENSUS REPORTED ON CONTENTS OF PRAYERS.**

**MORNING.** rain, thin as breath. the canal wears rings. At the park: you touch
it. it is warm. the rain does not bead on it the way it should.

At the flat, for once, the player doesn't sleep.

you don't sleep. you shouldn't do this. you do it anyway.

**DUSK.** the park at dusk is a different country.

the egg holds the last light longer than the sky does, the way a stone holds
noon.

the streetlamps come on. it ignores them beautifully.

you put your hand on it. warm. the same warm. day shift, night shift, no shift.

you go home the long way. bed argues. bed wins.

## NIGHT 13

marisol asks you to open the window. "i want to hear the rain."

it isn't raining.

you open it. she listens to the rain that isn't there.

"there it is," she says.

you sit by the open window. the not-rain falls and falls. her son comes at
midnight. you leave them the room.

**"EGG TOURS" LAUNCH IN FOUR CITIES. CRITICS NOTE PROXIMITY IS FREE.**

**MORNING.** The park. The bench. Home. Sleep.

## NIGHT 14

marisol's bed is empty. the window is open. you left it that way.

the lilies are still there. someone should take them home.

you take them home.

**OFFERINGS REPORTED AT OBJECT SITES: FLOWERS. LETTERS. A RING.**

**MORNING.** the walk home is longer when someone has died. The hand on the
warm metal. The stairs you don't remember.

---

*three nights pass.*

## NIGHT 17

there is a new name: dee. dee is twenty-six.

dee wants a different room. a different window. a different jello. a different
body.

the anger is doing a job. you know what job.

"and get me a proper pillow," she says. "these are bricks."

You bring her the pillow from the empty bed. "it's the same," she says. it is.
she keeps it.

dee wants the door closed. you close it.

**GLOBAL SLEEP QUALITY IMPROVING, SAY RESEARCHERS. CAUSE UNCLEAR.**
**THE QUESTION "WHY HERE?" ASKED IN EVERY LANGUAGE. ANSWERED IN NONE.**

**MORNING.** By now the walk is just: the canal path. At the park it's the
regulars now. the dog man. the two nurses from st. anne's — the other shift.
nods all round. the coffee cart man knows your order.

---

*four nights pass.*

## NIGHT 21

dee has a list tonight. she reads it out. doctors, foods, gods, weather.
everything is on it.

"and you," she adds, not looking up.

"fair," you say.

she almost smiles. it costs her something. she pays it.

**FIRST WEDDING HELD "BY THE EGG." THE WORD HAS STUCK.**
**OFFICIALS PREFER "THE OBJECTS." OFFICIALS ARE LOSING.**

**MORNING.** rain again. the canal wears rings. you touch it — the rain does not
bead on it the way it should — and then you sit on the bench and go very still,
longer than you ever have. And for the first time, after most of a minute of
unbroken stillness:

*not a sound. a feeling in the teeth.*

*the way a cathedral hums after the organ has stopped.*

(From this sit onward, whenever the player is still at the egg, a low 68 Hz
hum fades in under everything. It was always there. Now they can hear it.)

---

*the nights run together.*

## NIGHT 25

dee's mother visited. the room still rings with it.

"she wants me to fight," dee says. "everyone wants me to fight. like i'm losing
on purpose."

you don't answer. you pull up the chair.

"you're still here," she says. it is almost an accusation. it is almost thanks.

**TEENAGERS USING OBJECTS AS MEETING POINTS. PARENTS APPROVE OF THE LIGHTING.**

**MORNING.** At the park:

a woman stands close to it. closer than people stand.

she is talking, low. you catch one word: a name.

she presses something small to the metal, holds it there, and sets it down at
the base.

she walks past you. "forty years," she says, as if you'd asked. "he'd have
loved this thing."

at the base, catching the streetlight: a ring.

You walk her to the gate. she talks about him. you listen. it costs nothing.
it isn't nothing.

---

*a week passes.*

## NIGHT 30

dee is small tonight. the list is gone.

"can you just sit," she says. "not do anything. don't even talk. just sit."

The controls go away again.

you sit.
the ward hums its one note.
her hand finds the edge of your sleeve. not holding. just touching.
minutes pass.

"okay," she says, finally. "okay."

**VIGIL CROWDS THIN. ORGANIZER: "IT'S NOT OVER. IT'S JUST NOT NEWS."**
**STUDY: SURFACE TEMPERATURE CONSTANT IN ALL CLIMATES. "HELD-HAND WARM," SAYS AUTHOR, THEN APOLOGIZES.**

**MORNING.** you touch it. it is warm. and — new, tonight —

somewhere under your palm: attention.

---

*a week passes. it does that now.*

## NIGHT 36

dee's bed is empty.

on the pillow — the one you brought her — a note in biro: "thanks for nothing."

and underneath, smaller: "i mean it. the nothing. thanks."

the new name is joan. joan is knitting.

"i'm doing the staff in order of kindness," she says. "you're next but one."

you sit. the needles keep time. it is a very good clock. "next but one," she
says again, and counts a row.

**ONE IN THREE CITY DWELLERS NOW PASSES AN OBJECT DAILY. MOST NO LONGER PHOTOGRAPH IT.**

**MORNING.** The park. The hum. The cold, eventually, in your coat.

---

*a week passes.*

## NIGHT 43

joan's hands don't stop. wool the color of good tea.

"my husband never sat still either," she says. "i knit, he paced. forty years
we wore out one carpet between us."

you sit. row by row, something grows out of nothing. it's a trick you never
tire of. she measures it against her arm, frowns, unravels an inch, is happy.

**LAGOS VIDEO PASSES ONE BILLION VIEWS: "THEY'RE NOT WAITING FOR US TO FIGURE OUT THE MESSAGE. WE ARE THE MESSAGE."**

**MORNING.** rain, thin as breath. the canal wears rings. You stop at the park.
You sit on the bench, in the rain, and are still — longer than the cold should
allow. The hum in your teeth. And then, once, only once, ever:

*a second band of words rises under the first,*

*the way frost grows on a window from the inside.*

*it does not pass through language at all.*

**THANK YOU FOR NOT FLINCHING.**

**THERE IS NO HURRY.**

**WE HAVE ALL THE TIME YOU NEED.**

*then it fades.*

*you will never see it again.*

you walk home without noticing the walk.

---

*a week. another.*

## NIGHT 50

joan has finished something small. she holds it up: a hat. sized for nothing
human.

"for the egg," she says. "for winter. don't laugh."

you don't laugh.

you sit, turning the little hat over in your hands. it is very well made.
"go on home," she says. "some of us have knitting."

**LAGOS TEENAGER DECLINES ALL INTERVIEWS: "I SAID IT ALREADY."**

**MORNING.** At the park, the player finally uses the verb that appeared after
the widow: *leave something.* what you have is what you have:

you fit the little hat to the top of it. it takes some reaching.

it fits. joan would be furious with pride.

it looks small. everything does, next to it. it stays.

---

*weeks pass.*

## NIGHT 60

joan's bed is empty.

on your locker, wrapped in tissue: mittens. a note: "you were first, really.
don't tell."

**FIRST SNOW ON THE NORTHERN OBJECTS. THE PHOTOGRAPHS ARE THE WHOLE STORY.**

---

*the weeks pass. winter comes early that year.*

## WINTER, YEAR 1

the ward gets its winter cough. extra blankets. the radiators tick like slow
clocks.

new names come and go on the doors. you are gentle with all of them.

**ONE YEAR ON: SCHOOLS REPORT CHILDREN "SIMPLY USED TO THEM."**

At the park: winter has the park to itself. the egg wears the cold like it
wears everything: without comment. the bench is yours.

Walking around it, a second time, the game plays one quiet trick:

you notice the crocuses at the base — wait.

there are no crocuses yet.

there is nothing at the base but frost.

The flat, by now, is just: the kettle. the bed.

## SPRING, YEAR 2

*the season turns.*

a woman in room two keeps her curtains open all night.

"i paid for the sky," she says.

fair.

**BIRDS NEST ON THE WARM ONES, ORNITHOLOGISTS CONFIRM. EARLIER BROODS. LOUDER SPRINGS.**

crocuses at the base, now, really. someone planted them. or no one did. either
way. the egg. the bench. the canal.

## SUMMER, YEAR 2

*the season turns.*

the ward in summer: fans, ice chips, the smell of cut grass through the one
good window.

**EGG COVERAGE MOVES TO WEATHER SEGMENT.**

an old man does tai chi by the water now, mornings. slow as the canal. the egg.
the bench. the canal.

(After this, the ticker goes blank. It stays blank. The news has finished with
the egg; the story hasn't.)

## AUTUMN, YEAR 2

*the season turns.*

a man in room six shows you card tricks with hands that shake.

the trick still works. maybe better.

At the park — the egg. the bench. leaves. — and:

the thing you left is gone.

taken by weather, or a thief, or — you like to think — accepted.

## WINTER, YEAR 2

*the season turns.*

there is a man in room four with no visitors and no name anyone uses. the quiet
man.

he watches the door. when you come in, he watches you. that is all the
conversation there is.

you sit. he watches you a while. then he watches the window. the two of you
divide the room's work between you. when you stand to go, he has already gone
back to the door.

## SPRING, YEAR 3

*the season turns.*

the quiet man is thinner. the window is doing most of the talking tonight:
rain, a gull, rain again.

he moves two fingers on the blanket, a small wave, like dealing a card. you sit.

rain, a gull, rain again.

## AUTUMN, YEAR 3

*the seasons turn. you stop counting.*

the quiet man's breathing has changed.

you know this music by now.

you sit. the machines tick. he doesn't need you to say anything.

minutes pass.

at the door you look back. he is watching the window. the window is doing fine.

---

*winter. then, the way it does, all at once:*

## SPRING, YEAR 4

the quiet man's bed is empty. you open the window yourself. it's what you do
now.

the shift is over.

spring. the canal path. you have walked this walk two thousand times.

the water still holds the light flat. some things hold.

the tai chi man is there, early, moving like weather.

crocuses at the base, thick this year. someone must add bulbs every autumn.

or no one does.

you sit on the bench.

the bench knows your shape.

the canal. a gull.

the hum you know is there whether you can feel it or not.

you know what it would say.

somewhere out past everything, the long hello continues.

*there was never any hurry.*

---

The only option left is: **begin again.**

## Appendix: what varies between playthroughs

- **The second message** (`THANK YOU FOR NOT FLINCHING...`) is the game's one
  earned secret: it requires ~15 minutes of cumulative real stillness at the
  egg across 8+ visits, having sat with at least four patients, and having made
  no demands ("ask it") in the last five park visits. It shows once per
  *machine*, ever — replays that re-earn it get only: *it is warm. once is all
  anyone gets.* A player who never earns it gets a different penultimate line
  in the ending: *you never did touch it for long. it stands there anyway.
  patient as weather.*
- **The hum** is missable the same way; discovered from night 21 by long
  unbroken stillness.
- **What you leave** at the base: the coffee, a coat button, joan's hat (only
  if night 50 was reached), or a line the player types themselves onto a
  luggage tag. If the hat is never left, it turns up on the player's
  windowsill in autumn of year 2 instead ("it fits the kettle, near enough").
- **Dialogue choices** never branch the plot; some quietly raise an internal
  "ease" stat. The patients die on schedule regardless. That is the point.
- **"ask it"** exists as a verb from night 8 and silently disappears in act
  III — the world stopped demanding.
- **The final line** (*there was never any hurry.*) only appears if the player
  once sat still on the title screen long enough to be told *there is no
  hurry.*
